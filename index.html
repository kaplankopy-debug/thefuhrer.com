Poland Campaign, 1939

Germany’s invasion of Poland began on 1 September 1939 and marked the opening of the European war. German forces attacked from multiple directions, aiming to destroy Polish armies near the frontier before they could regroup or establish a longer defensive line in the interior.

Map of the 1939 invasion of Poland

Overview

The campaign combined rapid armoured thrusts, close air support, and coordinated pressure from north, west, and south. The operational aim was to break Polish mobilisation, encircle major field forces, and seize key political and transport centres before an organised long-term resistance could be established.

Polish forces fought a determined defence, but they faced severe problems: incomplete mobilisation, vulnerable frontier deployment, weaker air cover, and pressure on multiple axes at once. The Soviet invasion from the east on 17 September further destroyed Poland’s strategic position.

Operational Design

Northern Axis

German forces advanced from East Prussia and Pomerania, helping cut the Polish Corridor and link separated fronts.

Western Axis

The main pressure came from western Germany toward central Poland, targeting communications, transport lines, and major army formations.

Southern Axis

Attacks from Slovakia and Upper Silesia supported deep penetrations toward Kraków, Lwów, and the Vistula line.

Encirclement Aim

Rather than merely pushing Polish armies back, the German concept aimed to trap and destroy them in pockets before they could retreat eastward.

Key Facts

Start Date
1 September 1939
End Date
Early October 1939
Main Result
Defeat and partition of Poland
Strategic Effect
European war begins

Outcome

Poland was overrun within weeks, though organised resistance continued in places after the main field campaign had collapsed. The victory demonstrated the effectiveness of concentrated mechanised operations against an opponent that was strategically exposed and not fully mobilised.

Politically, the invasion triggered British and French declarations of war against Germany, transforming the conflict from a regional crisis into a general European war.
