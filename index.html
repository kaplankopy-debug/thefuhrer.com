Invasion of Poland (1939)

The invasion of Poland began on 1 September 1939 and marked the opening major campaign of the Second World War in Europe. German forces attacked from multiple directions, aiming to destroy Polish armies near the frontier before they could fully regroup or establish a prolonged defensive line in the interior.

Overview

Germany’s attack on Poland was designed as a rapid, coordinated offensive using armoured formations, motorised units, infantry, artillery, and air power to break Polish resistance quickly. The campaign sought to exploit mobility, local concentration of force, and repeated encirclement of Polish field armies. The invasion also had a decisive diplomatic effect: Britain and France declared war on Germany on 3 September 1939.

The Polish armed forces fought a determined defensive campaign but faced severe strategic disadvantages. Germany held numerical and material advantages in many key categories, enjoyed air superiority early in the fighting, and attacked from several axes. Once Soviet forces entered eastern Poland on 17 September 1939, Polish prospects collapsed further. Organized resistance continued into early October, but the campaign was effectively decided by late September.

Military Resources Used

German Forces

  • About 1.5 million personnel committed to the campaign.
  • Roughly 2,000 to 2,800 tanks and armoured fighting vehicles, depending on how they are counted.
  • Thousands of artillery pieces and extensive motor transport to support rapid operational movement.
  • Large Luftwaffe commitment for close air support, interdiction, reconnaissance, and attacks on infrastructure.
  • Attack launched from Germany proper, East Prussia, and areas linked to Slovakia, creating multiple pressure points.

Polish Forces

  • Roughly 1 million mobilised personnel, though full mobilisation was incomplete when the attack began.
  • Much smaller armoured and motorised capacity than Germany.
  • Heavy reliance on infantry formations, horse-drawn transport, and dispersed mobilisation plans.
  • Air force and anti-air resources were limited relative to the scale of the German offensive.
  • Defensive planning was complicated by the need to cover long frontiers and protect key cities and industrial areas.

Course of the Campaign

1 September 1939
German forces invade Poland from north, west, and south. Air attacks and fast-moving ground offensives aim to disrupt mobilisation and fracture Polish command coherence from the outset.
First week of September
German armoured and motorised units achieve rapid breakthroughs. Polish forces fight a series of defensive engagements, withdrawals, and local counterattacks while trying to avoid encirclement.
9–19 September
The Battle of the Bzura becomes the largest major Polish counteroffensive of the campaign. Although it temporarily disrupts German operations, it is ultimately contained and defeated.
17 September 1939
Soviet forces enter eastern Poland, destroying any remaining realistic prospect of sustained strategic resistance by the Polish state.
Late September
Warsaw comes under intense siege and bombardment. The capital surrenders on 28 September. Major organised resistance continues only in isolated areas.
Early October 1939
Remaining Polish formations capitulate. The campaign ends with Poland partitioned between Germany and the Soviet Union.

Outcome

Result
German and Soviet victory; Poland partitioned
Duration
1 September – 6 October 1939
Strategic Effect
Triggered the wider European war
Operational Pattern
Rapid penetration, encirclement, dislocation

Immediate Consequences

  • Poland was overrun and divided between Germany and the Soviet Union.
  • Britain and France declared war on Germany, transforming the conflict into a major European war.
  • The campaign demonstrated the operational impact of combining armour, air power, artillery, and speed.
  • It also exposed the vulnerability of a state forced to defend long borders without equivalent mobility or air cover.

Historical Assessment

The 1939 Polish campaign is often treated as the first major demonstration of the style of warfare that would later be associated with early German successes: concentrated force at decisive points, rapid movement, close cooperation between ground and air arms, and relentless efforts to disrupt enemy command and retreat routes. That said, historians also stress that the campaign’s outcome depended on Poland’s exposed geography, incomplete mobilisation, the diplomatic situation, and the subsequent Soviet intervention.

In military terms, the invasion showed how quickly a modern campaign could be decided when one side achieved operational superiority in movement, coordination, and air support. In political terms, it destroyed the European balance that had survived the interwar period and began the continental war that would expand into a global conflict.
